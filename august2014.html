Neighbors Oppose 43-Unit Development
TUESDAY, JUNE 3, 2014:


Area neighbors again face plans to develop the hillside at Eastern and Lombardy, this time from Newport Beach-based Clearwater Properties. The potential buyer proposes constructing 43 ‘view homes,’ each with a two-car garage, on the site. Opponents say El Sereno, with its open hillsides and lack of a responsible growth plan repeatedly falls prey to ambitious developers’ oversized projects. While master-planned communities can be great money-makers for developers, they don’t ever successfully blend into established neighborhoods. Whether the sale will be finalized is uncertain. Area neighbors, pointing to the wildly popular Arroyo playground, suggest the property could better serve the community as a public space. “The owner might not know it’s an option,” said one community member, “But selling to the city or a conservation agency is a sure bet, while sales for development often fail in the eleventh hour.” For more information, go to 90032.org/land-use

Less than 1% Vote in Local Election

About 0.9% of those eligible voted in the recent LA32 Neighborhood Council (NC) election. Approximately 33,000 people over the age of 18 live in 90032 and may vote for Neighborhood Council members. An additional, variable population of out-of-the-area stakeholders who own or do business, shop, or have some stake in the community are also eligible to vote. How many people actually made it to the polls? 302 people voted on a Saturday, April 26th at Farmdale Elementary. According to former treasurer, Anthony Manzano, EmpowerLA, the city office that oversees NCs allowed LA-32NC to spend approximately $10,000 for election outreach. The purpose of the NC is to address the concerns of our neighbors, to make impartial decisions that benefit the majority, and be the voice of our community to city government.

Lincoln High Softball – A Community Gem

By Vera Padilla The Lincoln High School softball team has become a source of pride for the Lincoln Heights community for the past 17 years. With an LAUSD Championship (2007) and an 85% winning percentage in the tough Northern League, the Tigers have a group of outstanding young ladies and parents who are a credit to the neighborhood. The Tigers have been a constant top ten ranked team in Division I softball in Los Angeles. In the 2013 season, Lincoln softball was ranked number four of 98 teams in Los Angeles. Currently, Lincoln is a top eight team in the city. The discipline and work ethic the players develop playing softball carry over to the classroom and to academic success. Of the 73 players who have graduated since 1999, 94% have continued their education in two and four year colleges and universities. Graduates have gone on the become doctors, engineers and teachers among other careers. As the Lincoln High program has progressed, the success of its athletes has translated into opportunities to play on the college level. More than one half of the Lincoln High graduates have played on the next level. East Los Angeles College, Glendale College, Valley College, Pasadena College, Santa Monica College and Cerritos College have had numerous Lincoln graduates play for them. Four year universities in which graduates have received scholarships include Morgan State, Howard University, Maryland Eastern Shore, Cal State Dominguez Hills, Sierra University and Mayville State University. This past year, Angelica Felix received a scholarship and play for the UCLA Bruins. Quite an accomplishment! The community of Lincoln Heights is proud of the accomplishments of the Tigers softball team. Where ever the Tigers travel to play, the sense of pride and achievement are always recognized within the softball community.

Last Call for Poetry

By Natan Solis


Readers at The Writer’s Underground open mic recite poetry from memory, from notebooks or off their phones. Each person attends the monthly reading at the Eastside Cafe for a different reason. Some are new to poetry and this is their first push into the open water. Others have shaken off any sense of [MK1] timidity from countless other readings and reveal their entire self to the room of strangers.
“I’m sure it saved my life,” said Jeffrey Martin of the power of poetry. Last Thursday night, he read an original piece about prayer, anger and frustration from his Baptist upbringing. His cadence is fast paced, and he often pauses from his reading to give asides on his work, about his life and how he became the man he is today.
As far as open spaces go, the Eastside Cafe is a Swiss Army Knife of community centers. Narcotics Anonymous, ESL classes, Son Jorocho lessons and many more public services dot the calendar at the space, all organized by a series of collectives and individuals. Eastside Cafe borrows from the Zapatista Movement, relying heavily on donations and shying away from grants. During The Writer’s Underground, a donation box is filled about halfway with singles, and, by the end of the night, there are few open seats available.
The audience is seated in a crescent shape and the room lit by strands of Christmas lights. From the street corner, a traffic light illuminates readers in the room with green-yellow-red patterns. William Gonzalez bangs on an empty wooden box he brought from home, and his percussion seems to only make the room grow more intimate.
Naomi Molinar traveled by bus from Upland to El Sereno to read her poetry in a somber voice that grew bold and vibrant during her time at the mic. Sophia Rivera, in her first reading at the monthly event, read one of her poems about being true to herself.
Poet and host Iris De Anda began The Writer’s Underground in January of this year, and she remarks that the group is growing steadily with newer readers at the mic.
“Even though the Eastside may have other places for people to go to, there’s always a need to have places that are in walking distance,” says De Anda, who began the evening by reading her poem Blood Moon.
“We wanted to open the space to as many people as possible, who may have never had the opportunity to express themselves,” said De Anda, who has lived in El Sereno for most of her life.
With the sign-up sheet exhausted, De Anda scans the room for any remaining readers calling out “OK. Last call for poetry.”
The Writer’s Underground open mic happens the third Thursday of every month at the Eastside Cafe 5469 Huntington Dr., Los Angeles, CA 90032

Climbers Find a Stronghold in Brewery Arts Complex

By Marni Epstein
Stronghold Climbing Gym has recently placed a firm grip on the old Edison Electric Company space at the Brewery Arts complex. This Lincoln Heights space is a Los Angeles Historic-Cultural Monument originally designed by Los Angeles architect, John Parkinson (who also designed City Hall, Bullocks Wilshire, and Union Station). The 13,000-square-foot climbing gym now housed within is the passion project of newly-married husband and wife team Kate Mullen and Peter Steadman of nearby Hermon.

Mullen, a computational physicist and Steadman, who has a background in civil rights law, have both enjoyed climbing for the last twenty years. They now look to impart their love of the sport to others in the area. “We hope to provide a positive outlet for the energy of many people in the Eastside community,” said Mullen via email. In fact, Mayor Eric Garcetti recently celebrated his birthday at the climbing gym.
Finding the ideal spot wasn’t without its challenges; a perfect storm of high ceilings, appropriate zoning, parking, and a good location were needed. The couple is thrilled to be located among the dynamic artist community of the Brewery Arts complex. No doubt the discipline and strength the couple have cultivated over their time rock climbing will be tested as they balance their first business endeavor with new-found married life.

Nueva Presidenta de LA32 Empieza

La recién elegida presidente del Consejo Vecinal LA- 32, Marleen Fonseca, reconoce los desafíos que enfrenta y la importancia de reiniciar el experimento cívico en el empoderamiento comunitario. Debido a que los fondos del NC han sido congelados durante 10 meses, su primer objetivo es traer a LA- 32 hasta el cumplimiento de modo que puedan utilizar los $37,500 asignados anualmente por la cuidad. Ella planea trabajar con su diversa consejo de colegas para formular metas medibles y para fomentar una mayor participación cívica para asistir a reuniones y unirse a los comités. Nacida y criada en Hillside Village, un graduada de la Universidad de California Riverside, y un propietaria local desde 1997, Fonseca está criando a sus dos hijos adolescentes en esta comunidad. Ella planea usar sus 15 años de experiencia trabajando con la cuidad y su red de contactos para el mejoramiento de esta comunidad. Ha trabajado para la ciudad de Los Angeles aplicando las normas de la ciudad, las políticas y procedimientos para garantizar el cumplimiento de los acuerdos laborales por los más de 300 empleados en su departamento, trabajando con todos los sindicatos de trabajadores municipales para resolver y negociar las condiciones de empleo y discrepancias. Ella ahora trabaja como representante de los trabajadores y representa a miles de empleados en seis departamentos de la ciudad. Además, ella es miembro del Comité de Gestión Conjunto Laboral Beneficios con más alto funcionario administrativo de la ciudad, administradores municipales y los sindicatos para tomar decisiones que afectan a las prestaciones médicas para todos los empleados de la ciudad.

New LA32 President Hits Ground Running

Newly elected LA-32 Neighborhood Council President Marleen Fonseca recognizes the challenges she faces and the importance of rebooting the civic experiment in community empowerment.
Because the NC funds have been frozen for 10 months, her first focus is to bring LA-32 up to compliance so that they may utilize the $37,500 allocated annually by the City. She plans to work with her diverse board of colleagues to formulate measurable goals and to encourage greater civic engagement to attend meetings and join committees.
Born and raised in Hillside Village, a graduate of UC Riverside, and a local homeowner since 1997, Fonseca is raising her two teenage children in this community. She plans to use her experience of working 15-years with City Hall and her network of contacts for the betterment of this community.
She has worked for the City of Los Angeles applying city rules, policies and procedures to ensure compliance with labor agreements for her department of over 300 employees, working with all of the City labor unions to resolve and negotiate employment conditions and discrepancies. She now serves as a Labor Representative and represents thousands of employees in six city departments. Additionally, she serves on the Joint Labor Management Benefits Committee with the city’s Chief Administrative Officer, city managers and unions to make decisions that affect medical benefits for all city employees.


Architecture with a Story in Lincoln Heights

By Marni Epstein
Talk of suicide, a family feud and a courtroom battle over a father’s will — that’s enough intrigue and noir to fill a Raymond Chandler novel. In a classic setting like the Di Blasi Office Building in Lincoln Heights, how could one expect anything less? The two-story brick building at 2714 North Broadway in Lincoln Heights retains much of its vintage character, including the letters “D” and “B” that have been combined to form a symbol that is repeated in decorative panels across the facade of the building. In the 1920s, this is where Angelo Di Blasi ran a successful produce business – Angelo Di Blasi & Sons – that became the subject of a family and courtroom battle.
Angelo Di Blasi ran his business with sons Carmelo and Antonio. The sons’ association with the business came under much scrutiny, however, after Angelo’s death in June 1929.
Antonio, the elder son, sued his brother and mother, Concetta de Luca Di Blasi, to dissolve a partnership and account for the estate left by his father, according to a story from the Los Angeles times in 1930. “The plaintiff asserts that after his father’s death, his mother and brother drove him from home,” said the story. Antonio, who claimed half interest in the business, maintained that his father referred to him as “the boss.”
There was also mention in the trial coverage of the elder Di Blasi’s suicide, which was rumored to have been precipitated by “his favorite son” Carmelo’s incessant canoodling with a certain girl of whom the family apparently didn’t approve. Carmelo, though, denied that his affairs were to blame for his father’s death.
The truth behind Angelo’s death remains unclear but his elder son, Antonio, emerged victorious in court, which broke his father’s will and named him a partner in the business.

Today, the building that bears the Di Blasi Office Building is home to the Lincoln Heights Chamber of Commerce, a women’s fashion boutique, and other merchants. While the Di Blasi business may have dissolved into an ugly family drama, an attractive two-story landmark remains in the heart of the Lincoln Heights Broadway business district.

LAPD-Neighborhood Watch Program Watching Out for You

By Mark Overstreet, CPAB Member
Problems can creep into a neighborhood. Complaining does nothing to solve them.
But being vigilant, knowledgeable about what can be done, and taking action can prevent situations from growing into problems.
If you believe the area where you live should be safe, civil and clean, then be a part of the solution. The Los Angeles Police Department sponsors Neighborhood Watch Programs throughout Los Angeles. Neighborhood Watch is one of the most effective and least costly ways to prevent crime. Participants work with an assigned LAPD –Senior Lead Police Officer to identify ways to improve neighborhood safety and also work with the City Council Office and other community agencies. Participants volunteer time and energy, and forge bonds with their community as they look out for their neighbors and themselves.
Local residents report great things about their Neighborhood Watch Programs. Ken Kallman of the Hermon neighborhood watch says “We have monthly neighborhood clean-ups. It sends a message to others with less than genuine intentions (i.e., graffiti, shopping cart abandonment, minor vandalism).” The group works on public safety and quality of life issues, emergency preparedness and businesses that are not well suited to the residential community (e.g. dispensaries). Ken says, “We coordinate our efforts through our Senior Lead Officer, the Department of Transportation, and the Fire Department.”
Mark Overstreet founded the University Hills neighborhood watch program 23 years ago. The group’s work over the years has resulted in street repaving, N.W. block parties, Beautification Days, University Hills signs, additional stop signs, a flag pole and landscaping at the neighborhood entrance, elimination of drug houses, and the list goes on. Mark stated that sometimes the work gets tough, like ridding the area of the local gang and graffiti, but the positive results are well worth the effort.
Raelene Allard has been a long time member of the Hill Side Village’s watch group, one of the most organized in the area. They publish a quarterly newsletter and were successful in helping to get the Valley Railroad Overpass built. They hold annual Rummage sales, have community clean-up days and have held neighborhood block parties and plan to keep doing what they’ve been doing; protecting Hillside Village residents and property.
For more info: (Internet); Los Angeles Police Department Neighborhood Watch. Contact the Hollenbeck Community Police Station; 323-342-4100.
